Get started

1. Check GOV.UK Verify is right for your service

GOV.UK Verify could be right for you if your service needs to:

  • pay money to a user
  • have legal confirmation that a user is who they say they are, for example if they’re signing a mortgage deed
  • show a user’s personal information back to them, like their driving licence or passport details

You don’t need GOV.UK Verify if you only need to recognise a returning user. You can use services like GOV.UK Notify to help with authentication instead.

To help you decide if GOV.UK Verify is right for your service, you should think about:

  • what your service lets users do
  • whether you need to know the identity of your users, or whether knowing they are the same person every time will be enough
  • whether you need to be able to take users to court if they’ve been fraudulent
  • whether any other controls are in place to prevent fraud

Organisations that can use GOV.UK Verify include:

  • central government
  • local government
  • NHS
  • police

2. Understand how GOV.UK Verify works

GOV.UK Verify provides a ‘hub’ between government services and private sector suppliers who have all been assured to government standards. These suppliers (or ‘certified companies’) are:

  • Barclays
  • CitizenSafe
  • Digidentity
  • Experian
  • Post Office
  • Royal Mail
  • SecureIdentity

When a user tries to access your service:

  1. Your service sends them to the GOV.UK Verify hub.
  2. The hub sends them to a certified company.
  3. The certified company sends them back to the hub.
  4. The hub sends them back to your service.

What your users see

  1. Users will be asked if they already have an account with one of the certified companies.
  2. Users who already have an account just need to sign in.
  3. Users who don’t have an account can sign up online by answering questions only they know the answers to or by scanning their identity documents, for example a passport.
  4. Users will be sent straight back to your service.

Try the user journey

What your service receives

  1. The certified company will send you the user’s name, address and date of birth.
  2. You can match the user’s name, address and data of birth against records you already have, or create a new record to recognise your user in the future.

Which ‘level of assurance’

If you need to match a user to a real person, GOV.UK Verify provides different ‘levels of assurance’ (LOA) that describe how confident you need to be that your user is who they say they are. The most common ones are LOA 1 and LOA 2:

  • LOA 1 means the user might be who they claim to be
  • LOA 2 means the user is probably who they claim to be

When you decide which LOA to use, think about the consequences to your service and the person the user’s claiming to be.

Different parts of your service may need a different LOA. You should always use the lowest necessary LOA for any particular action.

LOA 1

Use LOA 1 when there wouldn’t be any serious consequences if a user impersonated someone else.

For example, if someone applies for a local resident discount for a toll crossing, the real resident can still apply for their own discount, and if they do they'll overwrite the previous application.

LOA 2

LOA 2 is usually appropriate if you’ll accept the user is who they say they are on the balance of probabilities.

LOA 2 is probably right for you if there would be a serious consequence to a user impersonating someone else on your service.

For example, if someone falsely submits a tax return in another person's name, that person could get a bill for money they don’t owe and need to spend time clearing up the error.

You can read more about levels of assurance in the Service Manual.

3. Share information with your team

Choosing to connect to Verify usually involves people with specialist knowledge, including technical, risk and privacy experts.

Share the GOV.UK Verify documentation with your team to help them understand what connecting to Verify involves.

4. Connect your service to GOV.UK Verify

To connect to Verify you’ll need to complete 6 steps:

  1. Proposal
  2. Needs analysis
  3. Planning
  4. Build and integration testing
  5. Connecting to production
  6. In beta

You can read about each step in the GOV.UK Verify documentation.
